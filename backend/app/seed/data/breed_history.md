# A History of the Wagyu Breed

## From Draft Animal to Global Delicacy

For most of its history, the animal we now call Wagyu was never bred for the dinner table. The word itself simply means "Japanese cattle" (*wa* = Japanese, *gyu* = cattle), and for centuries these beasts were prized as agricultural power — hardy, patient draft animals that plowed the terraced rice paddies of Japan. Their fine-grained, heavily marbled meat was a fortunate byproduct of a physiology built for stamina, not a deliberate goal. It was only in the 20th century, as mechanization freed the cattle from the fields, that Japanese breeders turned their full attention to carcass quality — and in doing so refined the most intensely marbled beef in the world.

## The First Four Bulls, 1976

The story of Wagyu outside Japan begins not with a shipment of thousands, but with a handful of bulls and one determined importer. In **1976**, Morris Whitney brought the first four Wagyu bulls to the United States. Their names — **Mazda, Mt. Fuji, Rueshaw, and Judo** — are foundational to virtually every full-blood pedigree traced in the Western hemisphere. (A common misremembering inserts a bull named "Mikimoto" into this group; the original four did not include him.)

Because so few animals came out of Japan in this era, and because there were no full-blood Wagyu females yet available abroad, these bulls could only be crossed onto other breeds. As a result, **every Wagyu animal outside Japan prior to 1993 was a percentage animal** — a crossbred carrying some fraction of Wagyu blood, never a purebred. The genetics existed, but the closed herd of true full-bloods remained locked inside Japan.

## The Import Wave of 1993–1997

The situation changed dramatically in the mid-1990s, in a narrow window that would prove historic. Between **1993 and 1997**, a series of carefully organized exports brought live full-blood cattle and genetics to North America and, from there, to Australia. This was the moment the modern global Wagyu industry was truly seeded.

Several ventures drove the wave:

- **Mannetti / World K's** imported influential Tajima and other black Wagyu genetics that remain central to elite marbling pedigrees.
- **JVP (Japanese Venture Partners)** and **Takeda Farms** brought additional black Wagyu bloodlines and foundation females.
- **The Wood family / HeartBrand** focused on the red strain — **Akaushi** — establishing the dominant Akaushi presence in the United States.
- Genetics that flowed onward became the basis of the Australian herd, later commercialized under names such as **Westholme**.

These imports finally delivered full-blood females as well as bulls, making it possible — for the first time — to breed purebred Wagyu on foreign soil.

## The Door Closes: The ~1997 Export Ban

The window did not stay open long. Around **1997**, Japan formally recognized Wagyu as a **national treasure** and effectively banned further live export of the cattle and their genetics. What had trickled out in the preceding years would become the entire foundation gene pool for the rest of the world. No significant legal export of live Japanese Wagyu has occurred since.

This is why the audited founder numbers matter so much. Careful reconstruction of the export records shows a total of **221 animals** leaving Japan across the relevant period — **188 black** and **22 red (Akaushi)**. Of these, roughly **183 were shipped live**, and the effective foundation breeding base narrowed further to an estimated **150 to 170 foundation females**. Every full-blood Wagyu bred in the West today descends from this remarkably small pool — a fact that shapes both the breed's prestige and its ongoing conversations about genetic diversity.

## The Legend of Kenhanafuji

No founder-era account is complete without **Kenhanafuji**, a bull whose reputation has grown into industry folklore. He reportedly changed hands for around **$150,000** — an eye-watering sum — earning him the affectionate nickname the **"International Bull of Mystery."** A popular story holds that he was purchased not for his own use so much as to *control his semen* and keep his genetics out of a rival's hands. That motive is best treated as **anecdotal**: it makes for a great tale, but it is not firmly documented, and readers should enjoy it as legend rather than settled fact.

## Quarantine: Separating Record from Myth

A colorful myth persists that the founder cattle spent **six months quarantined on an island near LaGuardia**. It is a vivid image — and it is not what the documented record shows. The **U.S. quarantine was approximately 30 days**, conducted at a mainland facility in New York State. The often-cited **180-day figure applied to red Wagyu bound for Canada**, under that country's separate import protocol, not to the U.S. animals. A refueling stop in **Anchorage, Alaska** is plausible as a logistical waypoint on the long trans-Pacific route, but it was a stopover, not a quarantine station. Accuracy here honors the real work of the importers rather than the embellishments that accrued afterward.

## The Four Bloodlines

Japanese Black Wagyu is organized into distinct **strains (bloodlines)**, each historically associated with a region and a breeding emphasis. Understanding them is the key to understanding Wagyu genetics:

- **Tajima (Hyogo)** — the marbling line. Prized for exceptional intramuscular fat and fine texture, though historically smaller-framed and slower-growing. This is the bloodline behind the most celebrated marbling.
- **Fujiyoshi / Shimane** — the balanced line, offering a middle ground between marbling and growth.
- **Kedaka / Tottori** — the growth-and-frame line, contributing size, structure, and larger mature weight.
- **Itozakura (also Shimane-associated)** — valued for growth combined with strong carcass performance.

Modern breeders blend these strains deliberately: Tajima for quality, Kedaka and Itozakura for size and yield, Fujiyoshi to balance the two.

## The Red Strain: Akaushi / Kumamoto

Alongside the black cattle stands the **Japanese Brown**, better known as **Akaushi**, historically centered in the **Kumamoto** region. Reddish-gold in color, Akaushi cattle grow well, marble impressively, and carry a reputation for favorable fatty-acid profiles. In the West, the Akaushi story is inseparable from the **Wood family and HeartBrand**, who built their program on the small group of red animals that made it out during the 1990s window.

## A Small Beginning, a Global Legacy

The global Wagyu industry — worth billions and spanning the United States, Australia, Europe, and beyond — rests on a founder population that could fit in a few pastures. From four bulls in 1976 to a tightly audited handful of animals in the 1990s, and then a closed door, the breed's spread is a story of scarcity, foresight, and careful stewardship. Every full-blood raised outside Japan today is a living link back to those few founders.

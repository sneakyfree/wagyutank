# A History of the Wagyu Breed

## From Draft Animal to the World's Most Coveted Beef

For most of its history, the animal we now call Wagyu was never bred for the table. The word itself simply means "Japanese cattle" — *wa* (Japanese) and *gyu* (cattle) — and for centuries these beasts were valued as living machinery: hardy, patient draft animals that pulled plows through the terraced rice paddies and hauled loads across mountainous country where no other power would do. Their fine-grained, heavily marbled meat was a fortunate byproduct of a physiology built for stamina and sustained work, not a deliberate breeding goal.

Isolation did the rest. Japan's mountainous geography kept regional cattle populations separate for generations, and for a long stretch of the country's history the eating of beef was discouraged or outright banned under Buddhist influence, so the cattle were shaped almost entirely by their working role and their local environment. Distinct regional strains emerged, each with its own conformation and temperament. It was only in the 20th century — as mechanization freed the cattle from the fields and Japan opened to outside breeds — that breeders turned their full attention to carcass quality, and in doing so refined the most intensely marbled beef in the world.

## The Four Strains — the DNA of Wagyu

Japanese Black Wagyu is not one uniform population but a blend of distinct **strains** (*keito*), each historically tied to a prefecture and a breeding emphasis. Understanding them is the key to understanding every modern pedigree, because breeders still blend these lines deliberately to balance marbling against growth.

- **Tajima (Hyogo Prefecture)** — the marbling engine. Compact, smaller-framed and slower-growing, the Tajima line produces the finest, most abundant intramuscular fat of any Wagyu strain, and it is the genetic foundation of Kobe beef. When people picture the impossible, snowflake marbling that made Wagyu famous, they are picturing Tajima. Its trade-off is size: Tajima cattle are lighter and yield less, so the line is prized for quality rather than quantity. Roughly half of the average full-blood genetics outside Japan trace to Tajima.

- **Fujiyoshi (Shimane Prefecture)** — the balanced line. Built largely on the legendary sire **Dai 7 Itozakura**, the Fujiyoshi strain offers a middle path: good marbling combined with genuine maternal ability, milk and moderate growth. It is the line breeders reach for to build productive cow herds rather than to chase a single carcass trait.

- **Kedaka (Tottori Prefecture)** — the growth-and-frame line. Originally bred as pack animals in the grain trade, Tottori cattle contribute size, skeletal structure, mature weight and feed efficiency while still holding respectable marbling. Kedaka blood is the classic outcross for adding frame and ribeye to marbling-heavy herds.

- **Itozakura (Shimane-centered)** — closely associated with the Fujiyoshi world and the second-most prominent line abroad, valued for combining growth with strong carcass performance and milk.

Alongside the Japanese Black stands the **Japanese Brown**, better known as **Akaushi** or Red Wagyu, historically centered in **Kumamoto Prefecture**. Reddish-gold rather than black, Akaushi cattle grow larger and faster, milk well, and carry a reputation for a favorable, higher-oleic fat profile and good performance on grass — with a lower marbling ceiling than the black Tajima line but exceptional balance.

## 1976: The First Four Bulls

The story of Wagyu outside Japan begins not with a shipment of thousands but with a handful of bulls and one determined importer. In **1976**, an individual named **Morris Whitney** — not, as often misremembered, a "Morris family" — brought the first four Wagyu bulls to the United States, the first Wagyu ever exported from Japan.

Two were Japanese Black: **Mazda**, a Tottori (Kedaka) bull whose descendants would later top modern marbling tests despite the growth reputation of his line, and **Mt. Fuji**, a Tajima bull from Hyogo prized for calving ease and maternal traits. Two were Red: **Rueshaw** and **Judo**, both from Kumamoto, the heart of Akaushi country. (The fourth bull is Judo — never "Mikimoto," a name that appears in no authentic record.) Rueshaw was no ordinary animal: the 1975 Japanese Red National Champion and, by repute, the only national champion ever permitted to leave Japan; he became the very first Wagyu registered with the American Wagyu Association, and a line-bred descendant would sell, decades later, for a Wagyu world-record half-million dollars.

Because there were no full-blood Wagyu females in America, these bulls could only be crossed onto Angus, Hereford, Holstein and Brangus cows while **Colorado State University** collected and studied their semen. A quirk of the record survives: the import paperwork reportedly described the bulls as Friesians. Whitney's venture underwhelmed commercially, and the four bulls eventually passed to a Texas veterinarian, Dennis Wendt of Georgetown. Their genetics endured all the same — Rueshaw's and Judo's through the entire American Akaushi population, Mazda's and Mt. Fuji's through the black herds — and their scarce semen still commands premium prices today.

## The Percentage Era

For nearly two decades after 1976, one fact governed everything: **there were no full-blood Wagyu females outside Japan.** Every Wagyu animal bred in America or Australia before 1993 was therefore a *percentage* animal — a crossbred carrying some fraction of Wagyu blood, graded up generation by generation, but never a purebred. The genetics existed abroad, but the closed herd of true full-bloods remained locked inside Japan. Everything changed in a single narrow window in the mid-1990s.

## 1993–1997: The Wave That Seeded the World

Between **1993 and 1997**, a series of carefully organized exports finally brought live full-blood cattle — bulls *and* females — out of Japan. This short window created the entire global full-blood Wagyu industry, and nearly every elite pedigree in the world today traces back to a handful of animals that moved in these few years.

**The Mannett Group (later World K's / Westholme)** led the way. In July 1993 the first shipment carried the bulls **Michifuku** and **Haruki 2** together with a small group of Tajima-influenced heifers — among them **Suzutani**, **Rikitani** and **Okutani** — initially through the Wagyu Sekai program in Canada. The first full-bloods ever born outside Japan came from this group; Okutani produced **Fujiko**, the first U.S.-born full-blood, in 1994. Michifuku would become perhaps the most influential marbling sire in Western history, and a second Mannett shipment in 1994 added the rare **Kenhanafuji** — the "International Bull of Mystery," a Fujiyoshi bull carrying no Tajima blood at all — along with **Takazakura** and more foundation females.

**Japanese Venture Partners (JVP)** imported another pillar group in 1994, headed by **Fukutsuru 068**, a pure-Tajima bull who would rank #1 for marbling in the U.S. sire summaries for fifteen straight years, alongside the massive **Kikuyasu-400** and **Yasutanisakura**.

**Takeda Farm**, the program of Japan's exacting breeder **Shogo Takeda**, exported its "TF" series of black bulls beginning in 1994 — including **Itomichi 1/2**, **Kikuhana**, **Itohana 2**, **Kinto** and **Terutani (TF40)** — together with roughly thirty-five black females, and later the marbling titans **Itoshigenami (TF148)** and **Itoshigefuji (TF147)**. Itoshigenami would go on to sire more registered progeny than any other foundation bull in Australia.

**The Akaushi line came entirely separately.** In 1994, **Dr. Al and Marie Wood** of Englewood Farm, Texas, imported the three Red Wagyu bulls **Shigemaru**, **Tamamaru** and **Hikari** along with a band of elite red females, founding what became **HeartBrand Beef** — the source of essentially all American Akaushi. This was the Woods and HeartBrand, not Takeda, and it is the single most-corrected error in Wagyu history.

Finally, in **1997–98, Chris Walker's Westholme program**, sourcing through the ET Japan Company in Hokkaido, imported the three "patriarch" bulls known by their tags — **Hirashigetayasu (001)**, **Itomoritaka (002)** and **Kitateruyasudoi (003)** — and routed the bulk of a large group of females onward to **Australia**, seeding that country's now world-leading herd.

## The Door Closes: The Export Ban

The window did not stay open long. Around **1997**, Japan formally recognized Wagyu as a **national treasure** and effectively banned the further live export of the cattle and their genetics. What had trickled out in the preceding few years became — permanently — the entire foundation gene pool for the rest of the world. No significant legal export of live Japanese Wagyu or its germplasm has occurred since.

This is why the founder numbers matter so much. Careful reconstruction of the export records puts the total at **221 animals** forming the genetic basis of all full-blood Wagyu outside Japan — **188 Japanese Black and 22 Red (Akaushi)** — of which roughly **183 were shipped live**, leaving an effective breeding base of somewhere around **150 to 170 foundation females**. Every full-blood Wagyu raised in the West today descends from this remarkably small pool. That scarcity is the source of both the breed's prestige and its most serious long-term challenge: the near-universal presence of a few key ancestors — the great sire **Yasumi Doi J10328** appears again and again across the top pedigrees — means the global herd carries a real genetic bottleneck that breeders actively manage through careful outcrossing.

## The Legend of Kenhanafuji

No founder-era account is complete without **Kenhanafuji**, the rarest of the imported bulls and, by reputation, the highest-selling Wagyu bull of all time — the "International Bull of Mystery." Genetically he stood apart from the celebrated Tajima marbling sires, carrying roughly three-quarters Fujiyoshi and one-quarter Kedaka blood and no Tajima at all, which made him a prized maternal sire producing large-framed, productive, feed-efficient daughters. Only a handful of North American ranches ever held semen on him, keeping straw prices extraordinarily high. The colorful tale that he was purchased specifically to *control his genetics* and keep them from a rival is the kind of story the Wagyu world loves — best enjoyed as legend rather than documented fact.

## Separating Record from Myth

One persistent myth deserves correcting: the story that the founder cattle spent six months quarantined on an island near LaGuardia. It is a vivid image, and it is not what the documented record shows. The U.S. quarantine for the 1993 black-Wagyu shipment ran roughly **thirty days**, at a mainland facility in New York State. The often-cited **180-day** figure belonged to a *different* group — red Wagyu bound for Canada under that country's separate protocol. A refueling stop in **Anchorage, Alaska** is entirely plausible on the long trans-Pacific route, but it was a stopover, not a quarantine station. Accuracy here honors the real work of the importers rather than the embellishments that accrued afterward.

## A Small Beginning, a Global Legacy

The global Wagyu industry — worth billions and spanning the United States, Australia, Europe, South America and beyond — rests on a founder population that could have fit in a few pastures. From four bulls in 1976, through a tightly audited handful of animals in the 1990s, to a door that closed and never reopened, the breed's spread is a story of scarcity, foresight and careful stewardship. Every full-blood raised outside Japan today is a living link back to those few founders — the marbling of Michifuku and Itoshigenami, the maternal strength of Kenhanafuji and Itomoritaka, the championship blood of Rueshaw — carried forward in every straw, every embryo, and every calf.

# The Murray Grey Story

Every great cattle breed has an origin. Most were engineered on purpose. The Murray Grey began as an accident — a run of odd silver calves on a single station beside an Australian river — and it took a woman who refused to see them as a mistake to turn that accident into one of only two beef breeds developed entirely in Australia.

## An accident on the Murray

The story begins at **Thologolong**, a grazing property on the upper Murray River, straddling the New South Wales–Victoria border about 65 kilometres east of Albury–Wodonga. The land had belonged to the **Sutherland family**, and Peter Sutherland had been breeding Aberdeen Angus there since the 1880s.

Around the turn of the century — most sources say **1905**, though the breed's own Australian associations date the event slightly later, to about **1914** — a light **roan Shorthorn cow**, kept on the station as a house cow, was mated to Sutherland's black Angus bulls. Instead of the expected black or roan calves, she threw an unbroken run of **silver-grey calves**. Accounts differ on the exact number — **twelve** in the breed's own careful histories, thirteen in the more widely repeated retellings — but they agree on the important part: the grey colour *bred true*. It reappeared, generation after generation.

Nobody quite knew what to call them. The Sutherlands nicknamed them **"Mulberries."** Later they were **"Goldens."** One neighbouring breeder, Mervyn Gadd, wondered whether a Galloway somewhere in the past explained the diluted coat, and called them "Grey Galloways." The name that finally stuck came from the market, not the paddock: when the cattle began topping the Newmarket saleyards in Melbourne in the **late 1940s**, they needed a name a buyer could shout. "Murray" for the river they came from. "Grey" for the coat. **Murray Grey.**

## The woman who made a breed

A curiosity is not a breed. What turned these silver cattle into a herd with a future was **Helen Player**, an English-born visitor to Thologolong in the 1920s who took a liking to the grey calves that others still regarded as an oddity. In **1931** she married **Keith Sutherland**, of the Thologolong family, and when the station's herd was dispersed at the **1932 sale**, Helen bought **eight foundation females** and began the first deliberate, systematic breeding program — selecting for fertility, mothering ability, growth, carcase merit, structure, and the quiet temperament the breed is still known for.

It was not easy country or an easy time. The **1945 drought** reduced her herd to just eight survivors. She rebuilt. She was later granted her own herd book under the prefix she chose, and in **1971** the Murray Grey Beef Cattle Society made her a Life Member for what she had done. A granite monument stands at Thologolong today, unveiled in 1991, carrying words from her own diary: *"This breed by accident was nature's, which in turn is God's gift to our land Australia."* The breed's founders across three countries still credit Helen Sutherland as the woman who made the Murray Grey.

## Proving it in the paddock — and the ring

While Helen refined the seedstock, a second nucleus proved the cattle could pay. In the late 1930s, **Mervyn Gadd** acquired a grey Thologolong bull — offered, the story goes, "for the price of a steer" — and ran it over thirty commercial Angus females as a hard-nosed commercial trial. When his grey-cross steers topped the Newmarket sales, the industry took notice. The Murray Grey had proven it could do the one thing a commercial breed must: turn grass into money.

The reputation went international in **1967**, when Murray Grey carcases placed **first, second, and third** in the Commonwealth Carcase Competition at London's Smithfield Show — the breed's carcase-quality breakthrough on the world stage, and a milestone still cited more than half a century later.

In **1962** (some records say 1960), around fifty breeders met and formed the **Murray Grey Beef Cattle Society of Australia**, giving the breed its founding herd book. In 1981 the separately-developed **Tasmanian Grey** — another Australian silver breed, begun in 1938 from unrelated foundation stock — merged under the Murray Grey banner, uniting the country's grey cattle.

## Around the world

From its single Australian river valley the Murray Grey spread across the cattle-raising world:

- **United States** — the first Murray Grey semen arrived in **1969**, live animals in the early 1970s, and the **American Murray Grey Association** formed in **1971**, based in Ohio.
- **New Zealand** — societies formed in 1970–72; the first mainland-Australian cattle shipped to New Zealand in ninety years, in October 1971, were Murray Greys. New Zealand breeders came to prize the breed's resistance to **facial eczema**.
- **United Kingdom** — imported in the early 1970s; the UK Murray Grey Cattle Society marked its 50th anniversary in 2023.
- **Canada**, and smaller footholds in **South America**, **South Africa**, and **Europe**.

Australia remains the breed's heartland — its country of origin, home of the founding herd book, the largest breeder base, and the BREEDPLAN genetic infrastructure that underpins the breed worldwide. What Japan is to the Wagyu world, Australia is to the Murray Grey: the source, and the place the rest of the world still looks to.

## What a Murray Grey is

More than a century of selection produced a breed with a recognisable set of virtues:

- **The silver coat** — ranging from bright silver through grey and dun to milk-chocolate, over dark, pigmented skin. The colour gene is strongly dominant, so even crossbred calves come out grey — making Murray Grey influence instantly visible in a commercial herd.
- **Naturally polled** — hornless, with the polled gene dominant, so calves come polled even over horned cows.
- **Docility** — cited in every serious account as a hallmark. Quiet, easily handled cattle, prized by cow-calf and feedlot operators alike.
- **Calving ease and mothering** — moderate birth weights, safe over heifers, good milk.
- **Grass efficiency** — a reputation, since the Gadd era, for finishing on pasture without a feedlot, which places the breed squarely in the modern grass-fed market.
- **Marbling and carcase quality** — the breed's signature commercial claim, and the reason for that 1967 Smithfield sweep.
- **Climate hardiness** — the lighter coat sheds heat, while the dark skin beneath resists eye cancer, sunburn, and photosensitivity.

## A breed at the right moment

For decades the Murray Grey was a quiet achiever — respected by those who ran it, under-marketed to everyone else. That is changing. As the grass-fed and grass-finished market grows, a moderate-framed, docile, efficient breed that marbles off pasture is exactly what a new generation of producers is looking for. The community around the world is growing — and, for the first time, has a single place to gather.

*A note on the record: like most breeds born before formal herd books, the Murray Grey's earliest history survives in several versions. We've followed the breed's own associations where sources conflict — noting, for instance, both the popular 1905 and the associations' later 1914 origin dates, and the twelve-calf figure its careful histories prefer — because a community that knows its own story deserves an honest telling of it.*
